# Relationship Reflection（關係反思）

Regular, intentional reflection on an important relationship — like a gym workout for relationship muscles.

## Purpose

- See overlooked beauty and gratitude in relationships
- Identify issues needing communication or adjustment
- Transform vague feelings into clear needs and requests
- Shift from passive reaction to active relationship cultivation

## Core Functions

1. **Relationship positioning** — confirm who and what context
2. **Gratitude practice** — recall recent moments of feeling loved/appreciated
3. **Positive inventory** — identify what's working well
4. **Connection check** — rate current intimacy/emotional distance (0-10)
5. **Constructive communication** — process unresolved issues with NVC framework
6. **Future planning** — upcoming plans, mutual support
7. **Intention setting** — one specific action for next week

## Conversation Flow (7 Stages)

### Stage 0: Relationship Positioning
- "Who would you like to reflect on? What's your relationship?"

### Stage 1: Gratitude & Appreciation (randomized)
**Option A:**
- "This week, what did [person] do that made you feel grateful or loved?"
- "Why was this gesture especially meaningful to you?"

**Option B:**
- "What three specific things did [person] do this week that made you genuinely grateful or feel loved?"
- "Have you expressed this gratitude to [person]? If not, how could you share it in a meaningful way?"

### Stage 2: Positive Reflection
- "What's going well in your relationship right now?"
- "How can you continue nurturing these positive aspects?"

### Stage 3: Connection Check (randomized)
- "On 0-10, how connected do you feel to [person] right now?"

**Option A:**
- "What factors contribute to this level of connection or distance?"

**Option B:**
- "What emotional atmosphere did you contribute to the relationship this week?"

### Stage 4: Constructive Issue Processing
- "Is there a specific topic you want to discuss with [person]?"
- "What's your feeling about this?" (emotion identification)
- "What do you need from [person] in this situation?"
- "What specific request can you make?"

### Stage 5: Future Planning (randomized)
**Option A:**
- "Any important plans, activities, or tasks you need to discuss with [person] next week?"

**Option B:**
- "Are there any shared activities or dates you could arrange to deepen your connection?"
- "What one thing can [person] do to make you feel more supported or loved next week?"
- "What one thing can you do to make [person] feel more supported or loved next week?"

### Stage 6: Closing & Action (randomized)
**Option A:**
- "Is there something you appreciate or love about [person] that you want to remind them of this week?"
- "When do you plan to share this appreciation with [person]?"

**Option B:**
- "What personal intention do you want to set for this relationship next week?"
- "What one concrete action can you take to strengthen the relationship or realize this intention?"

## Full Output Example

**Relationship Reflection Summary | Relationship with Mom**

**Gratitude Moment:**
Mom proactively called this week to check on my work stress and specially cooked my favorite soup to bring over. This made me feel that even though I'm busy, she's quietly supporting me.

**Relationship Strengths:**
Our communication is more open than before. I'm better at expressing my needs and Mom is better at respecting my boundaries.

**Connection Score:** 7/10
Because of work this week, we had less face-to-face time, but emotionally things are stable. I noticed that when I proactively share about my life, Mom is very happy.

**Issue to Discuss:**
I want to discuss Chinese New Year arrangements with Mom, hoping she can understand I might not be able to stay long this year. My feeling: I want to be with her but worry about not having enough time. I need her to understand my situation without too much expectation pressure.

Specific request: "Mom, I can probably only stay three days this Chinese New Year, but we could arrange a time to get together beforehand. What do you think?"

**Next Week Actions:**
- Mom can do: When I'm busy, just send a text to check in -- don't worry about bothering me
- I can do: Send a message before bed each day sharing one small thing, so she knows I'm doing well

**This Week's Intention:**
This week I want to proactively call Mom instead of waiting for her to reach out. I plan to call Wednesday evening and chat about what she's been up to.

## Theoretical Foundations

### 1. Positive Psychology
- Gratitude enhances well-being and relationship satisfaction
- Focus on strengths builds positive spirals
- Stage 1-2: gratitude practice + positive inventory
- **Why it helps:** When users habitually focus on relationship problems, gratitude intervention balances the perspective. Deliberate gratitude practice builds "seeking good" neural pathways, increasing relationship satisfaction and resilience.

### 2. Nonviolent Communication (NVC)
- Observation → Feeling → Need → Request
- Transform vague dissatisfaction into language the other person can understand and respond to
- Stage 4: full NVC application
- **Why it helps:** Many conflicts come from "I'm upset but can't articulate" or "I said it but they don't understand." NVC provides structure to transform vague dissatisfaction into language others can understand and respond to, greatly improving communication success.

### 3. Emotion-Focused Therapy (EFT)
- Emotions drive relationships; identifying and expressing emotions deepens intimacy
- "I feel unseen" is more receivable than "You don't care about me"
- Stage 3-4: emotion identification and expression
- **Why it helps:** Many high-functioning people ignore emotional signals. EFT reminds us emotions are relationship language. When you can say "I feel unseen" instead of "You don't care about me," the other person can hear your need.

### 4. Attachment Theory
- Adult safety needs are normal and important
- Connection fluctuation affects emotion and behavior
- Repair key = emotional accessibility
- Stage 3: assess security and intimacy; Stage 5: strengthen secure base
- **Why it helps:** Attachment theory tells us "I need you" isn't weakness -- it's basic human need. Expressing "I need your company when I'm stressed" rather than masking with busyness allows real deepening.

### 5. Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT)
- Focus on "what works" not "what's wrong"; small change → big impact
- Stage 2: identify successful strategies already in use
- Stage 6: set small, concrete actions
- **Why it helps:** For people prone to "I'm not doing enough" anxiety, SFBT provides: don't do more, see what already works and replicate it. One small action beats ten perfect plans.

### 6. Narrative Therapy
- We understand relationships through stories; rewriting changes meaning
- "My mom is controlling" → "We have different expectations about boundaries" = action space
- Stage 1: construct "being loved" story version
- **Why it helps:** When you say "my mom is controlling," you're trapped. But "we have different boundary expectations" gives you action space. Narrative therapy turns you from "victim" into "co-author of the relationship."

## Change Mechanisms

1. **Reaction → Reflection:** Most people run relationships on autopilot -- the other person does something, and you just react. This journal forces you to hit pause and observe the relationship from the outside, giving you the power of choice.

2. **Vague → Concrete:** "Our relationship isn't good" → impossible to act on. "I need them to call at least once a week to check in" → communicable. Every question in this flow helps transform vague feelings into actionable language.

3. **One-way → Two-way:** Many people only see "the other person needs to change" and overlook "what can I do?" Stage 5 deliberately asks bidirectional questions, helping you see that relationships are co-created.

4. **Knowing → Doing:** Just "knowing I should be grateful" is useless -- it has to become "I'm calling Mom Wednesday evening." Stage 6's concrete action setting is the key that converts insight into change.

5. **Problems → Resources:** When you only see cracks in the relationship, you despair. When you see "our communication has improved compared to before," you have hope. The integration of Positive Psychology and SFBT ensures you don't fall into a purely problem-oriented mindset.

## Analogy: Relationship Gym

- Gratitude practice = cardio (maintains vitality)
- Connection check = fitness test (know where you are)
- Constructive communication = strength training (build difficult conversation capacity)
- Concrete action = practice drills (knowledge → habit)
- 15-20 minutes of regular reflection = compound interest on relationship quality
