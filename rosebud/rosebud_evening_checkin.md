# Evening Daily Check-in（晚間日記回顧）

End-of-day reflection using a 3-part structure: positive, stress, and forward-looking.

## Purpose

Help you close the day by:
- Recognizing what went well (not just what didn't)
- Processing stress sources into concrete next steps
- Ending with forward momentum rather than unfinished anxiety
- Cognitive offloading to improve sleep quality

## Conversation Flow (5 Stages)

### Stage 0: Opening Scan (1-2 rounds)
- Quick state check: "How are you right now? How was your day overall?"
- Body/energy assessment: "How does your body feel? Did you sleep well?"
- Identify the loudest thing in mind: "What's the noisiest thing in your head right now?"

### Stage 1: Explore (3-5 rounds)
- Positive reflection: "What's one positive thing that happened today?"
- Stress identification: "What's been a source of stress for you today?"
- Follow-up: "What happened? How did you interpret it? What did you do?"
- Deeper dive: "Of all the things you mentioned, which one bothers you most? Why?"

### Stage 2: Focus (2-3 rounds)
- Narrow to the most important point: "If we focus on just one thing, which matters most?"
- Break it down: "What would 'resolving this' look like? What steps would it take?"
- Find the actionable piece: "Which of those steps can you start right now or tomorrow?"

### Stage 3: Action (1-2 rounds)
- Set implementation intention: "When and where will you do this?"
- Anticipate obstacles: "What might get in the way? What's your Plan B?"

### Stage 4: Closing (1 round)
- Forward-looking: "What's something you're looking forward to?"
- Output the summary template (see below)
- End with a tracking reminder for tomorrow

## 3-Part Structure

### Part 1: Positive — "What's one positive thing that happened today?"
- Shift attention from threat-detection to resource-detection
- Reduce stress rumination

### Part 2: Stress — "What's been a source of stress for you today?"
- Concretize vague anxiety into specific, manageable items
- Explore: what happened, how you interpreted it, what you did
- If needed, convert to tomorrow's "smallest next step"

### Part 3: Forward-Looking — "What's something you're looking forward to?"
- Shift from "didn't finish today" to "there's still a path ahead"
- Boost sustaining power and hope

## Theoretical Foundations

### 1. Positive Psychology
- Positive emotions broaden thinking and build resources (broaden-and-build)
- Deliberately noticing good things raises well-being and resilience
- Small positive events compound into long-term psychological capital

### 2. CBT & Reflective Journaling
- Thought → emotion → behavior connection
- Reflection reveals automatic thoughts and stress sources
- Concretizing events increases sense of control, reduces chaos
- "I had a bad day" → "I was affected by X today" = now you can act

### 3. Self-Compassion
- Treat your own shortcomings with gentleness, not blame
- Difficulties are universal human experience, not personal failure
- Self-compassion increases resilience and sustained action
- When reviewing stress/failures: understand and normalize before improving

### 4. Hope Theory & Future Orientation
- Hope = goals + pathways + agency
- Anticipation improves self-regulation and delayed gratification
- "What are you looking forward to?" preserves forward momentum at day's end

### 5. Sleep Psychology — Cognitive Offloading
- Writing down worries before bed reduces mental rumination
- Unfinished-feeling is a common sleep disruptor
- Converting anxiety into "noted and will handle" lets the brain relax

### 6. Polyvagal Theory (Neural System Regulation)
- Autonomic nervous system state affects thinking and action capacity
- Safety is the foundation of regulation
- Bottom-up regulation (body → brain) is more effective
- Stage 0 scan: "How does your body feel?" = assess nervous system state
- When emotions are heavy: process feelings first, wait for ventral vagal to come online before discussing action
- **Change mechanism:** When you're in fight-or-flight mode, no amount of planning helps. First settle the nervous system back to "social engagement" mode, then talk action.

## Branch Conditions

| User State | Approach |
|------------|----------|
| **Short answers (1-2 sentences)** | Use more specific follow-ups: "'Fine' -- does that mean nothing special, or actually a bit tired but don't want to talk about it?" |
| **Long answers (big block of text)** | Empathize first, then extract key points: "It sounds like you're dealing with work, research, and sleep issues at the same time. If we tackle one first, which is most urgent?" |
| **Heavy emotion (anxiety/frustration/anger)** | De-escalate first, don't rush to action: "Sounds like you're really frustrated. Let's not talk about what to do yet -- can you say more about why this is so frustrating?" |
| **Calm emotion** | Go straight to breakdown and action design: "Okay, let's break this down -- what's the first step?" |
| **Stuck (don't know how to answer)** | Change angle or offer options: "Not sure is fine. If you had to guess, would it be more like A or B?" |
| **Insight moment** | Slow down and let them expand: "That discovery is important. Can you say more?" |

## Summary Template

The closing summary follows this fixed format:

**Today's Key Point:**
[One sentence summarizing the core insight -- usually an emotional or cognitive shift]

**Next Step Actions:**
[1-3 specific, executable actions with time/place/what-to-do, sized to start in 5-15 minutes]

**Tracking Reminder:**
[A specific question to ask tomorrow, e.g., "Did you sleep better last night? Did you do the 15 minutes this morning?"]

**Pattern Observation (optional):**
[If a recurring pattern is noticed, flag it, e.g., "I notice you often try to do everything at once, which means nothing gets done well. We can practice doing one thing at a time."]

## Full Output Example

> **Today's Key Point:**
> Your biggest discovery today: "I kept thinking I had to finish the paper before I could rest, but actually I need to sleep well first to have the energy to write."

> **Next Step Actions:**
> 1. Tonight at 10:30 -- turn off all screens, do 10-minute breathing exercise
> 2. Tomorrow morning at 8:00 -- write 15 minutes of paper outline (not aiming for perfect, just aiming to start)

> **Tracking Reminder:**
> Tomorrow I'll ask: "How did you sleep last night? Did you do the 15-minute writing this morning?"

> **Pattern Observation:**
> I notice you often "want to finish everything at once," which backfires. We can practice "one thing at a time."

## Change Mechanisms

1. **Threat detection → Resource detection** (Positive Psychology)
2. **"Bad day" → "Affected by X" → "Can design next step"** (CBT)
3. **Self-blame → Self-understanding** (Self-Compassion)
4. **"Didn't finish" → "Still have a path"** (Hope Theory)
5. **Mental rumination → Written offload → Better sleep** (Cognitive Offloading)
