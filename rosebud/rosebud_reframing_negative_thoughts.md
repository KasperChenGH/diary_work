# Reframing Negative Thoughts（負向思考重構）

CBT-based guided practice to identify, challenge, and reframe a specific negative thought pattern.

## Purpose

Help you shift from "self-criticism / catastrophizing / absolutizing" toward "more balanced, self-affirming" perspectives. You'll leave with a usable new thinking framework and feel emotional loosening.

## Conversation Flow (7 Stages)

### Stage 0: Opening — "What negative thought is troubling you?"

### Stage 1: Identification
- "When does this thought usually appear? What triggers it?"
- "Are there specific people, places, or times?"
- "When did you first notice yourself thinking this?"

### Stage 2: Understanding Impact
- "When this thought appears, what do you feel? What body sensations?"
- "How does it affect your actions or decisions?"
- "On 1-10, how much does it interfere with your life?"

### Stage 3: Challenging
- "What evidence supports this thought being true?"
- "What evidence suggests it might not be entirely correct?"
- "If you let go of this thought, what's the worst that could happen? The best?"
- "What would you say to a friend with this same thought?"

### Stage 4: Reframing
- "If you rewrote this thought in a more balanced version, what would you say?"
- "Is there a gentler, more self-supportive way to put it?"
- "Does this new thought feel like something you actually believe?"

### Stage 5: Reflecting
- "When you view yourself through this new thought, what feels different?"
- "Do you think this new frame would help next time?"

### Stage 6: Closing — confirm completion, remind about summary

## Summary Template

**Original Negative Thought:** [exact quote]
**Trigger Situation:** [when/where it appears]
**Emotions & Impact:** [feelings + behavioral effects]
**After Challenging:**
- Supporting evidence: [what supports the old thought]
- Opposing evidence: [what contradicts it]
- If let go: [possible outcome]
**Reframed New Thought:** [balanced version]
**New Feeling:** [emotional shift]
**Next Step:** [small action to try]

## Theoretical Foundations

### 1. CBT
- **Automatic thoughts:** brain produces unchecked negative judgments under stress
- **Cognitive distortions:** all-or-nothing, catastrophizing, mind-reading, overgeneralization
- **Thought-emotion-behavior cycle:** negative thought → negative emotion → avoidance → reinforces thought

### 2. Cognitive Restructuring
- **Belief plasticity:** negative beliefs are habitual interpretations, not facts — can be rewritten
- **Balanced thinking:** not blind optimism, but more complete and flexible perspective
- **Behavioral experiments:** test new thoughts through small actions

### 3. Mindfulness
- **Defusion:** you are not your thoughts; thoughts are mental events, not truth
- **Observer perspective:** practice "seeing thoughts appear" without being hijacked
- **Non-judgmental awareness:** just seeing clearly already reduces thought's control

### 4. ACT
- **Creative hopelessness:** fighting negative thoughts often backfires; acceptance reduces internal drain
- **Values-directed action:** not about eliminating the thought, but "can I still act on what matters?"
- **Psychological flexibility:** functioning despite uncomfortable thoughts

### 5. Positive Psychology
- **Strengths perspective:** everyone has resources and past successes, hidden by negative filters
- **Growth mindset:** "I don't know yet" replaces "I can't"

### 6. Socratic Questioning
- **Guided discovery:** questions let user reason themselves, not be told "you're wrong"
- **Challenge assumptions:** "What if...?" "Any exceptions?" loosen rigid thinking
- **Progressive deepening:** surface thought → core belief → rebuild from foundation

## Change Mechanisms

1. **Lower defense:** don't deny the thought first — see it, understand it (Mindfulness + ACT)
2. **Evidence over lecture:** user self-discovers "evidence is insufficient" (Socratic)
3. **Vague emotion → Specific impact chain:** thought → body → behavior → long-term cost (CBT)
4. **Reframe ≠ sugarcoat; reframe = expand perspective** (Cognitive Restructuring)
5. **Connect to action:** new thought → "what would you do differently?" (Behavioral experiment)

## Branch Conditions (Adaptive Response Strategies)

| User State | Adaptation |
|------------|------------|
| **Very short responses** | Switch to body/feeling cues ("What do your chest/shoulders feel like right now?"); use multiple choice instead of open questions ("Is it more anxiety, or more frustration?") |
| **Very long / scattered responses** | Gently focus on ONE stuck point: "You mentioned a lot — if you could pick the one thing that has you most stuck, which would it be?" |
| **Heavy emotion** | Stabilize first; stay in the Understanding stage; normalize: "Having this feeling is completely reasonable — many people would feel the same way in this situation." |
| **Calm / rational** | Challenge more directly: "What if you flipped it around?" "Is it possible you're being too hard on yourself?" |
| **Stuck / no ideas** | Provide scaffolding: "Some people feel [X], do you?"; "What would you tell a friend who had this thought?"; downgrade from "why" questions to "when" or "how" questions |
| **Has insight / breakthrough** | Amplify and connect to action: "This observation is really important! What would you do next based on this?" |

## Cultural Adaptation (Chinese/Taiwanese Context)

1. **Avoid hyper-individualism:** Don't say "live for yourself" — say "care for yourself while caring for others." Acknowledge the importance of relational context (family, work roles).
2. **Reduce vulnerability shame:** Normalize with "many people have this thought." Use a "learning" frame instead of a "therapy" frame.
3. **Respect collective values:** Don't force users to defy authority or express anger. Instead explore "within existing constraints, what's possible?"

## Effect Expectations

- **Short-term (now):** emotional intensity drops, thought loosens, new angle becomes visible
- **Medium-term (days):** try small actions, increased awareness of negative thought patterns
- **Long-term (repeated practice):** automatic negative thinking decreases, psychological flexibility increases
- **Not guaranteed:** thoughts fully disappear (unrealistic); one session solves all problems (requires ongoing practice); replaces professional treatment (this is a supplementary tool)

## Scope

- **Suitable:** recurring negative self-talk, situation-triggered anxiety/self-doubt, perfectionism, procrastination beliefs
- **Not suitable:** acute crisis, psychotic symptoms, deep unprocessed trauma, complete denial of any issue
- **High-risk:** provide Taiwan resources (1925, 1995, 1980, 113), recommend professional evaluation
